# Case 3: The AI Distillation Controversy — When "Technology" Becomes "Crime"

## I. Event Background (February – June 2026)

In the first half of 2026, a fierce cognitive conflict erupted in the AI industry around "distillation":

- **February 2026**: Anthropic accused DeepSeek, Moonshot AI, and MiniMax of systematically distilling Claude model outputs through approximately 24,000 fake accounts and about 16 million interactions. Anthropic called this "massive IP theft." [TechCrunch, 2026-02]
- **March 2026**: OpenAI followed suit, naming DeepSeek as "heavily distilling GPT outputs" to train competing models. [Reuters, 2026-03]
- **March 2026 (same period)**: Cursor's Composer 2 was discovered by the community to be fine-tuned on Moonshot AI's Kimi K2.5, though the product page initially labeled it as an "in-house model." Cursor subsequently apologized, and user votes supported retaining Kimi integration. [The Verge, 2026-03]
- **April 23, 2026**: The White House released NSTM-4 (National Security Technology Memorandum No. 4), with "Anti-Distillation" in its title, characterizing distillation as "a threat to national security." [White House, NSTM-4, 2026-04-23]
- **May 2026**: Musk admitted in an interview that xAI also "heavily distills OpenAI outputs," but argued that "this is common industry practice." [Lex Fridman Podcast, 2026-05]
- **June 2026**: Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei alleged that distillation caused "Claude to degrade in professional domains" — a large volume of distillation requests lowered Claude's reasoning capability in medical, legal, and engineering domains. [CNBC, 2026-06]

## II. Conventional AI Analysis (Without MPT Framework)

> *"AI distillation is becoming a new battleground in US-China tech competition. Anthropic and OpenAI claim Chinese AI companies are stealing model capabilities through distillation — this is both an intellectual property issue and a national security concern. The White House's NSTM-4 memorandum shows the US government has brought distillation under policy regulation. However, distillation itself is common industry practice — even OpenAI has been exposed for distilling data from Google, and the Cursor incident reveals Silicon Valley's double standards. Musk's confession shows this is not just one company's problem but a systemic contradiction across the industry: when everyone does the same thing, why is only one side accused?"*

**This analysis "looks rational" — but what does it expose?** Let us dissect it layer by layer using the MPT v6 framework.

### 3.3 B1 Alienation Detection ⚠️ Core Diagnosis

**Three-Step Detection**:

1. **What is the category switch?** → "Calling an API" (A1 event) → "Calling an API a lot" (A1/A2, increased quantity) → "Malicious distillation" (B1 characterization) → "Intellectual property theft" (B1+B2 legal judgment) → "National security threat" (B1 political narrative). **Four layers of category leap, none examined at the A level.**

2. **Which A2 entity has the B1 symbol replaced?** → "Distillation" has replaced "API call data volume." The real A2 question is: Has the distilled party's model capability been physically transferred to the distilling party? This can be verified by running standard benchmarks (MMLU, GPQA, HumanEval, etc.) on both parties' models simultaneously. Yet no one in the public discourse has demanded this data be released.

3. **Is the inspection loop closed?** → Completely closed. Anthropic accuses → Media amplifies → US government regulates → Accused denies → Anthropic adds further accusation ("Claude's capability degrades") → More regulation. **No A-level data enters the B1→B2→B1 loop.**

**Diagnosis**: The **"distillation as theft" narrative is in a severe state of B1 Alienation.** A mundane A1-level API call behavior, through four consecutive category leaps, has been constructed as a B2-level "rule violation" and a B1-level "moral crime." Meanwhile, the real A2 question — "Does distillation actually transfer model capability? How much? What is the cost-efficiency ratio?" — has never entered public discourse. B1 symbols ("theft," "threat") have completely replaced the A2 entity (actual model capability transfer and its material impact).

### 3.4 Delta Analysis

- **A2 (Measurable capability of the distilled model)**: Anthropic claims Claude is degrading in professional domains. If true, the A2 Delta (model quality relative to R&D investment efficiency) is decreasing — **but is the degradation caused by distillation requests, or by a bug in the model architecture? Indistinguishable in practice.**
- **A2 (Measurable capability of the distilling model)**: DeepSeek/Kimi scores are rising on multiple benchmarks. If some of these improvements come from distillation, then the distilling party's A2 Delta (capability relative to cost) is expanding — **distillation creates a positive Delta at the A2 level.**
- **Delta Status**: This is an **asymmetric Delta** — the accused party's A2 Delta is expanding (increased model capability), while the accuser's A2 Delta is contracting (claimed "capability degradation"). Opposite Delta directions imply: the accuser has a B1 motivation to deviate from rational debate — attributing A2 efficiency loss to the other party's B2 violations rather than accepting A1-level technical competition.

### 3.6 Joint Three-Law Examination

| Three Laws | Examination | Result |
|------------|-------------|--------|
| **First Law**: Anthropic's claim that "distillation causes capability degradation" — can it be translated into a verifiable proposition? | Requires: 1) Claude capability evaluation data before and after degradation; 2) Time-series correlation analysis with distillation request volume; 3) Exclusion of other causal factors. | **Second Law**: Does Anthropic's "distillation = theft" framework include a path for self-correction? | Core framework assumption: "Using your own outputs to train competing models = illegitimate." This is the part of the case with the greatest MPT analytical value. Correspondence between the three B1 frameworks:

|------------|----------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Core Metaphor | Stealing | Learning/Reference | Attack |
| Moral Characterization | "Wrong" | "Neutral" | "Dangerous" |
| Source of Authority | Commercial rights | Technical tradition | National sovereignty |
| Standard of Verification | B2 (ToS) | A1 (Technical feasibility) | B1+B2 (Security + Law) |

**Key Finding**: The **ontological grounding** of these three frameworks differs — A anchors on B2 rules, B anchors on A1 technology, C anchors on B1 security. The same act — "training a model on API outputs" — is a "violation" in framework A, "reverse engineering" in framework B, and a "threat vector" in framework C. **This is not a disagreement about facts — it is three discussions at three different levels, using three different language systems, about three different questions.**

## V. MPT Operational Recommendations

1. **For the industry (general)**: Any public discussion involving "distillation" must explicitly label the discussion level (A1/A2/B1/B2). When you say "distillation is problematic," first ask yourself: Are you talking about an A1 technical issue, a B2 compliance issue, or a B1 moral issue? The answers at each level are entirely different.

2. **For Anthropic and OpenAI**: If "distillation causes capability degradation" is real, please make the facts and data public — Claude's time-series scores on MMLU/GPQA/HumanEval and correlation analysis. Otherwise, the First Law is not satisfied, and the accusation can only be treated as a "provisional hypothesis." Second Law self-reflection: Can your framework accommodate the counterfactual conclusion that "distillation's net effect on Claude's capability is negligible"?

3. **For Chinese AI companies**: Within the existing B2 rule framework, proactively initiate third-party independent evaluations — invite internationally recognized evaluation bodies to conduct blind comparisons of your models against Claude/GPT. If the A2 Delta (capability gap) has indeed narrowed, this is the strongest A-level defense and requires no B1 narrative. If the gap has not narrowed, the "distillation as theft" narrative collapses on its own.

4. **For policy makers (NSTM-4 related)**: The Third Law check is failed — rules must include a path for being challenged. Recommendations: 1) Specify NSTM-4's scientific review mechanism; 2) Add hearing procedures for regulated parties; 3) Define A2 triggering conditions for rule exit (e.g., "if independent evaluation shows distillation does not cause substantive capability transfer, regulation is automatically downgraded"). Power-Responsibility Parity check: If regulation is proven to be unnecessary ecological damage, who bears the responsibility?

5. **For the media**: When reporting on the distillation controversy, every B1 moral judgment term such as "theft," "stealing," or "threat" must be annotated with its operational definition — "'theft' here refers to API usage that violates Section X of the ToS, not theft as defined by criminal law." When the media uses B1 vocabulary without annotating its B2 reference, they themselves are participating in B1 Alienation.

6. **For independent researchers**: The greatest value of this case is that it is a **natural incommensurability laboratory**. The fact that three B1 frameworks construct the same A1 event differently demonstrates that there is no transparent channel between "facts" and "meaning." Researchers should collect corpus from all three frameworks, perform cross-annotation, and attempt to establish a common verification baseline at the A level (model capability evaluation).

## VI. Aggressive Negative Feedback Mechanism Design

This case exposes a complete failure of all three laws, making the design of Aggressive Negative Feedback especially critical:

**Core Aggressive Questions** (simulating self-examination):

- Questioning your own framework: "If distillation does achieve substantial model capability transfer (A2 evidence), does my 'distillation = theft' narrative still hold? Or does the question become 'is theft efficient'?"
- Questioning your own rules: "If the accused party voluntarily cooperates with independent evaluation and presents evidence that distillation produced no substantive transfer, would NSTM-4 automatically become invalid?"

**Mechanical Transmission Path** (prediction):

```
Current State: B1 Alienation (narrative replaces facts) → B2 Rule Expansion → C1 Discourse Power Consolidation → More B1 Narratives
     ↓ (if A-level data intervenes)
Corrected State: A2 Independent Evaluation → A-level Evidence Breaks B1→B2 Loop → B2 Rules Forced to Adjust → C1 Discourse Power Redistribution
```

**Predicting the mechanical transmission of regulatory pressure**: Violation of B2 rules (the accused continues distillation/circumvention) → B1 condemnation (the accuser escalates narrative) → If B1 is blocked by alienation (the two parties' frameworks are incommensurable) → Force accumulates at the A level (both parties accelerate R&D; model capability gap is determined by A2) → A-level eruption (a magnitude breakthrough by one party's model capability eliminates the necessity of distillation) → Repair is naturally forced (new rules are tighter than old, or regulation automatically expires).
